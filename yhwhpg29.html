In the song that we are about to talk about here, in the words to the song, Rescue, there is a mention of something called, SOS.
Although SOS, in popular usage, is an acronym commonly associated with phrases such as "Save Our Souls" and "Save Our Ship",
originally, SOS is a Morse code distress signal.
Morse code is a method used in telecommunication to encode text characters as standardised sequences of two different signal durations,
called dots and dashes, or dits and dahs.
Morse code is named after Samuel Morse, one of the inventors of the telegraph. He was born in 1791, and he died in 1872.
The American artist, inventor and painter Samuel Morse, the American physicist Joseph Henry, and mechanical engineer Alfred Vail developed an electrical telegraph system.
It needed a method to transmit natural language using only patterns of electrical pulses and the silence between them, sent back and forth across a simple electric wire. Around 1837, Morse, therefore developed an early forerunner to the modern International Morse code.
The Morse system for telegraphy, was used in about 1844.
Even the name used today, AT&T, stands for American Telephone and Telegraph Company.
The Western Union Company, used today for doing Wire transfers, Money orders and Money transfers, originally was known as the Western Union Telegraph Company.
The electrical telegraph was a point-to-point text messaging system, used from the 1840s until the late 20th century when it was slowly replaced by other telecommunication systems.
It was a way, back in 1832, to send messages across electric wires, before the invention of the telephone which was not until the year, 1876.
It took many years after that for the telephone to eventually become as popular as the telegraph.
Although commercial "telegraph" services are still available in many countries, transmission is usually done via a computer network rather than a dedicated wired connection.

SOS is a distress signal that means, Save Our Ship, and that you are in need of being rescued.
The pattern for the letter S, is long, long, long.
The pattern for the letter O is short, short, short.
So, as light, you can use a torch or flashlight.
With some of the new torches or flashlights today, you can press the power button a few times, and it will automatically flash the SOS light pattern.
You can also use a mirror and the reflection of the sun to signal the SOS light pattern.
As light, the SOS pattern would be, quick flash, quick flash, quick flash, pause, long flash, long flash, long flash, pause, quick flash, quick flash, quick flash.
If you were buried under something in an earthquake, and you could tap or bang on something loud so that people could find you,
the SOS as a sound pattern would be, tap, tap, tap, pause, pause, -- tap, pause; tap, pause; tap, pause; -- pause, pause, tap, tap, tap.
So, in summary, SOS is a Morse code distress signal, used when someone is marooned, trapped or stuck, and is in need of being rescued.

When I Read the Bible Through
is a poem by Amos Russel Wells
He was born in 1862, and he passed away in 1933.

Amos Russel Wells was a United States editor, author and a professor of Greek and geology at Antioch College from 1883 to 1891.
In 1891 he was also an editor of the Christian Endeavor World magazine and publishing company, in Boston.
From 1901, he was associate author of Peloubet's Notes on the Sunday School Lessons.
He was a member of the International Sunday-School Lesson Committee.

His works include books dealing with young people's work, the Sunday school, juvenile fiction, poetry and devotional literature.


He used the Christian Endeavor World, in Boston, to publish a book of his poems, in 1921.
The book is titled, "The Collected Poems of Amos R. Wells".
It has 257 pages, with illustrations by Hiram Putnam Barnes,
and one on page 34 by Charles D. Hubbard.
Page 249 to page 257, those last 9 pages, are an Index of all of the poem titles and topics.

Well, I ask you, what's wrong with me reading a book like that? I mean, after all, isn't that how we all read our Bibles?

In this poem is the English word, coruscant.
Most dictionaries do not even have this word listed at all.
It was eventually found in the Collins Dictionary.
In February 2022, I went to the Collins Dictionary to look up the word, coruscant.
And, according to the Collins Dictionary, coruscant, means,
giving off flashes of light.
So, when you read the part in the poem that says, Imperial Jeremiah, With his keen, coruscant mind;
I think the author is trying to say that Jeremiah has a mind that thinks as fast as a flash of lightning.
But the Bible does not really say that.
This has nothing to do with Coruscant, the planet that has the same name in the fictional Star Wars universe.

Here is the poem, titled,
When I Read the Bible Through
as found on Page 8 of the 1921 book, "The Collected Poems of Amos R. Wells"
written by Amos Russel Wells.


I supposed I knew my Bible
    Reading piecemeal, hit and miss,
Now a bit of John or Matthew,
    Now a snatch of Genesis,
Certain chapters of Isaiah
    Certain Psalms (the twenty-third!);
Twelfth of Romans, First of Proverbs --
    Yes, I thought I knew the Word!
But I found that thorough reading
    Was a different thing to do,
And the way was unfamiliar
    When I read the Bible through.

Oh, the massive, mighty volume!
    Oh, the treasures manifold!
Oh, the beauty of the wisdom
    And the grace it proved to hold!
As the story of the Hebrews
    Swept in majesty along,
As it leaped in waves prophetic,
    As it burst to sacred song,
As it gleamed with Christly omens,
    The Old Testament was new,
Strong with cumulative power,
    When I read the Bible through.

Ah! Imperial Jeremiah,
    With his keen, coruscant mind;
And the blunt old Nehemiah,
    And Ezekiel refined!
Newly came the Minor Prophets
    Each with his distinctive robe,
Newly came the Song idyllic,
    And the tragedy of Job;
Deuteronomy, the regal,
    To a towering mountain grew,
With its comrade peaks around it,
    When I read the Bible through.

What a radiant procession
    As the pages rise and fall,
James the sturdy, John the tender
    O the myriad-minded Paul!
Vast apocalyptic glories
    Wheel and thunder, flash and flame,
Whilst the church triumphant raises
    One incomparable Name.
Ah, the story of the Saviour
    Never glows supremely true
Till you read it whole and swiftly,
    Till you read the Bible through.

You who like to play at Bible,
    Dip and dabble, here and there,
Just before you kneel, aweary,
    And yawn through a hurried prayer;
You who treat the Crown of Writings
    As you treat no other book --
Just a paragraph disjointed,
    Just a crude, impatient look --
Try a worthier procedure,
    Try a broad and steady view;
You will kneel in very rapture
    When you read the Bible through!
