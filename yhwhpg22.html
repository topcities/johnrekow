Feast 3 of 7.
To calculate this date for Feast 3 of 7, we start with the day of Passover, also known as First day of Passover (Day 1).
Starting with the day after Passover Day 1, the next day, you wait until the day after the next Sabbath Saturday, which will land on a Sunday.
That next Sunday is Feast 3 of 7. The Sunday feast actually starts at sunset on the previous Saturday night.
To summarise this again,
1. You start with the day after a Passover.
2. You wait until after the next Saturday comes and goes.
3. On that next Saturday, at sunset, is the start of Feast 3 of 7, that continues on into that Sunday, ending on that Sunday sunset.




If Passover starts on sunset of a Saturday, then that means that the Passover is on a Sunday, and so you wait until the day after the following Saturday, at the end of the next week, and then on that following Saturday at sunset is the start of Feast 3 of 7.
If Passover starts on sunset of a Friday, then that means that Passover is on a Saturday Sabbath, and so that Saturday evening, at sunset, Feast 2 of 7 starts.
But, the counting does not yet start for Feast 3 of 7. In order for the Sunday to qualify for Feast 3 of 7...
1. Passover must come and go first.
2. The next Saturday must come and go, and at sunset on that next Saturday is Feast 3 of 7.

Again, you wait until the day after Passover Day 1, and then wait until the following Saturday at sunset to start Feast 3 of 7 which continues into Sunday, until Sunday sunset.

Here is a little bit of background about that song, coming from Wikipedia, back in August 2021.

"I'll Fly Away", is a hymn written in 1929 by Albert E. Brumley and published in 1932 by the Hartford Music company, in a collection titled, Wonderful Message.

It is often considered to be the most recorded gospel song of all time.
It's inspired from an earlier nineteenth century slave song, which were songs that slaves would sing whilst working.
Some of the words from that slave song, about a bird flying away from a prison, were made popular again in a secular song that was released about five years before Albert E. Brumley wrote this song.
That is what inspired this song, which is why it contains a part that talks about a bird flying away from a prison.
The prison would be, being stuck in this earthly life, and wanting to escape from this world and be set free.

Since 1940, many people have made recordings of this song over the years.
There are also instrumental recordings, and the song can be heard in several films and TV shows.
It was heard in two episodes of The Waltons.
There was even a TV series made at one time, from 1991 to 1993. The TV series was named after the hymn.
Those that try to list all of the known recordings of this song will typically end up with a list of over 300 recordings.
Some artists made another, second recording of this song again, years later.
