Christian Essay:  Best Seller

I recently went to the New York Time's Best Seller List, and near the top of the list was this book:
"The Girl in the Spider's Web", by David Lagercrantz.

So I bought it and put it on my coffee table.

The next day, I picked it up, flipped to a random page near the beginning, and read one sentence, then I put it down.
That was a very well-written sentence, I said to myself.

The next day, I saw it there again on the table, so I picked it up and read about 3 or 4 sentences from some random page somewhere in the middle.
I was thinking, Wow!  This author certainly knows how to write a good story!

The next day, I saw it there again, and I was kind of wondering how the story ended, so I picked a random page near the back of the book and read two whole paragraphs at one time. That was most of one page!
Wow!  That was amazing and exciting!  This author sure knows how to write!  Maybe I'll read another entire random paragraph tomorrow!

By now you're probably asking yourself, what's wrong with this guy?  That's not how you're supposed to read a book!
You're supposed to start at the beginning and read each page completely all the way to the end! How can you get all that the book has to offer when you only read little bits and pieces, here and there, randomly like that?

Well, I ask you, what's wrong with me reading a book like that?  I mean, after all, isn't that how we all read our Bibles?

If we look at the original Greek word in Acts 2:42, according to the Strong's Greek Definition of Fellowship,
The original Greek word for fellowship was "koinonia."
koinonia means, fellowship, communion, communication, distribution, contribution, to communicate,
association, community, joint participation, intercourse, share which one has in anything, participation, intimacy...
a gift jointly contributed, a collection, a contribution.

koinonia comes from koinonos.
koinonos is a partaker, partner, companion, associate, a sharer in anything, sharing in the worship.

The Chambers 21st Century Dictionary describes Fellowship as "friendly companionship."

Notice that "assembling" and "together" were both originally the same Greek word, "episunagoge."

Strong's says that episunagoge means, "gathering together, assembling together, a gathering together in one place, the (religious) assembly (of Christians)."

Commentary: The Bible shows us what the original Christian church service looked like and what it consisted of. We have four main components that made up the original church service. Fellowship is one of those four key ingredients. The other three would be Reading the Bible, the Breaking of bread, also known as the Lord's Supper or Communion, and lastly, praying. The Bible does a good job telling us about the importance and value of reading the Bible, the symbolism and importance of remembering to do Communion, and we have a significant amount of advice, instruction and importance about praying and how to pray. The one thing that is not so easy to find and extract from the scriptures are instructions and examples of fellowship. Fellowship sounds like Gathering Together, meeting together, and being together as a group of people, but there are two different Greek words, koinonia and episunagoge. If I try to bring all of these words and meanings together, it seems that fellowship is when fellow Christians come together and spend time together, communicating and sharing their needs and what they are going through. I once heard that when you are going through something, a friend is someone who goes through it with you. There seems to be an intimate sort of intercourse, and a sharing together. NLT mentions "fellowship meals." Fellowship does not just happen with fellow Christians. There also seems to be a sort of fellowship that happens with all of the Christians and Jesus and the Holy Spirit. What would happen at a fellowship meal? We all take in the same thing together as one. The same food all enters into us as though we are all one body. We have fellowship with each other and also fellowship with Christ and fellowship with the Holy Spirit. It comes back to communion where Jesus wants to enter into you and become one with you as At-One-Ment, or atonement.

In conclusion, do all four of these essential components of what a church service should be as it was originally defined in the book of Acts, and that includes doing communion "often" and fellowshipping before and after the service. Even if you are a pastor and you need to catch a plane after the service or you need to get to a wedding or funeral, don't make a fast exit out the door, because fellowshipping is part of the church service, and is just as important as praying, reading the bible and doing communion at a church service.
