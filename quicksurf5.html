About The Bible

The Holy Bible that is commonly found today consists of 66 books, which are divided into two sections.

In the front section, the first 39 books make up what is known as the Old Testament, which were books written before Jesus Christ was born. Of those, the oldest book in your Bible would be the book of Job (pronounced, "jobe"). We do not know exactly when Job was written. Many Bible scholars agree with the more commonly accepted assessment that the book of Job may have been written around the time of 1500 BC, which is about three thousand, five hundred and twenty-four (3524) years ago, when counting back from the year of 2024 AD. Some have speculated that it may be about sixty to one hundred years newer than that, while others estimate that it might be anywhere from 300 to 600 years older than that.

The second section, towards the back of the book, are the books of the New Testament, which consist of the remaining 27 books of the bible. The New Testament books were all written in the decades after Jesus Christ died. Jesus died in the year 33, and the last and newest book of the New Testament, also the last book at the end of the Bible, is the book of Revelation, which was mostly likely written in the year 96, but it could be even a year or two older than that. If the year of 96 is correct, and we count back from the year of 2024 AD, that would mean that the book of Revelation was written about one thousand, nine hundred and twenty-eight (1928) years ago.

The Old Testament books were originally written in Hebrew, and the New Testament books were originally written in the Greek language. There have been five noteworthy occasions when a group of scholars each spent a number of years translating the entire Bible into English. As a result, it is academically acknowledged that the following five English translations are acceptable as being accurate English versions of the ancient texts:

King James Version (KJV) (1611)
New King James Version (NKJV) (1982)
New International Version (NIV) (1978, with revisions published in 1984 and 2011.)
New Living Translation (NLT) (1996, with revisions published in 2004, 2007, 2013 and 2015.)
New American Standard Bible (NASB) (1995)


These English translations are the most accurate and the most recommended for your Bible studies, especially the KJV and NLT most of all.
Reading the KJV and the NLT together, at the same time, can be one of the best ways to do a Bible study.
Other interesting and noteworthy English translations to consider...

The problem with this translation is that the Old English may be difficult to read and understand.



There are several other English translations currently available, but they may not be considered to be as accurate. Some are known to be quite inaccurate and should be avoided.


Each translation took a series of years to complete by each group of scholars. Some portions were released in the few years immediately before the above years shown. The years shown above are the years when each translation was completed and made available in its entirety.

There are at least five different editions out there of the New Living Translation
First edition: 1996
Second edition: 2004
Third edition: 2007
Fourth edition: 2013
Fifth edition: 2015

The first edition is still considered to be a fairly good translation, but the newer editions are additional attempts to help improve the accuracy and precision of the NLT.
The NLT is not just a word-for-word translation, but a thought-for-thought translation, trying to help give more thoughtful meaning and understanding of what was trying to be originally communicated.

Different people all reading from the NLT may not each see the exact same wording in the NLT, due to the multiple revisions published.

The NIV was updated in 1984 and 2011.
Some have rejected the NIV 2011 update because of gender-neutral language.
If different people are studying together in the NIV version, they may encounter differences in the wording due to the different NIV editions.


Even though there is now a 2020 edition of the New American Standard Bible (NASB), when we choose to occasionally quote the NASB, we will be quoting from the previous, 1995 edition, unless we specifically say otherwise.
