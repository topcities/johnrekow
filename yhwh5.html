The hymn,
Amazing Grace
was published in February 1779,
and was intended to be a spiritual autobiography of its author, John Newton.

John Newton was born in 1725, and died in 1807 at the age of 82.
He was an British Anglican minister of the Christian church, who served as a sailor in the Royal Navy, and later as the captain of slave ships.
After his retirement, he later regretted and rejected slavery after witnessing the horrors of it firsthand.

In February 2019, this hymn was 240 years old.

The hymn was published in a hymn book, Olney Hymns, in February 1779.
It contained the works of John Newton
and his poet friend, William Cowper (1731–1800).

The city of Olney is a market town and civil parish, with a population of about seven thousand, in the Borough of Milton Keynes, in Buckinghamshire county, in South East England.


When Amazing Grace was originally used in the city of Olney, it was unknown what music, if any, accompanied the verses written by John Newton.

The hymnbooks of that day did not contain music and were simply small books of religious poetry.

The words of Amazing Grace were inserted into a few different songs until it was finally joined into the song, New Britain, in 1835.

The song, "Amazing Grace" that is commonly sang today, is sang to the tune of the song, "New Britain" by William Walker, and this musical version was first published in a hymn book out of Philadelphia, in, The Southern Harmony and Musical Companion, in 1835.

The first six verses were first published by John Newton in 1779,
The last verse, that starts with, When we've been there ten thousand years,
was added later on, by Harriet Elisabeth Beecher Stowe.
No one knows who first came up with that last verse.
She had heard that additional verse being passed down orally in African American communities,
and she first had it published in 1852, when she included it in her anti-slavery novel, Uncle Tom's Cabin.

It's interesting how, the original author, and the author who added the last verse, 73 years later, were both opposed to slavery.

The Prayer of Saint Francis


The Prayer of Saint Francis



Here is an interesting prayer.
This prayer is not in the Bible, but it is a good prayer to read.
This prayer was first published in a magazine in France in the year 1912, but nobody knows who first wrote it.
The magazine's copyright has expired.
Many people call this the Prayer of Saint Francis, but the actual person, the Italian Saint Francis of Assisi, died about 686 years earlier, in the year 1226.

Assisi is a city and small area in the central, north east area of Italy.
Saint Francis did not write this prayer.

Since the prayer was originally published in French, here is the English translation.

Lord, make me an instrument of your peace.
Where there is hatred, let me bring love.
Where there is offence, let me bring pardon.
Where there is discord, let me bring union.
Where there is error, let me bring truth.
Where there is doubt, let me bring faith.
Where there is despair, let me bring hope.
Where there is darkness, let me bring your light.
Where there is sadness, let me bring joy.
O Master, let me not seek as much
to be consoled as to console,
to be understood as to understand,
to be loved as to love,
for it is in giving that one receives,
it is in self-forgetting that one finds,
it is in pardoning that one is pardoned,
it is in dying that one is raised to eternal life.

The Rescue from The Wicked and Evil


The Rescue from The Wicked and Evil



Commentary:
There was a season when King Saul, possessed with many demons, especially, fear, torment, anger, pride and jealousy towards David, was trying to kill David before he became the next king. During that time, David was in hiding, knowing that all of King Saul's army was searching for David, in order to kill him. It was during these stressful times when David wrote several of the Psalms that we now have today. Keep that in mind every time you see a Psalm that begins with the words, A Psalm of David. I imagine that David was battling spiritual attacks, like rejection, depression, and fear, and he did not have any anti-depressants to help level him off. Imagine what his Psalms would have been like if he was on anti-depressants; Psalms that probably would end up being not as good or as true or as helpful for us today. That would explain why David would write in a Psalm his desire to see his enemies defeated, disabled, and destroyed. That is one perspective that we see in Psalms 109.

But Jesus also offers a different, alternate perspective in regards to our attitude towards our enemies. David is saying here in Psalms 109 that he wants his enemies disabled or destroyed so they will stop trying to kill him. Here is a different perspective that Jesus teaches. Let us consider both David's perspective, and Jesus' teachings when it comes to our choices, our prayers, and our actions and attitudes towards our enemies.


David is asking the Lord to curse his enemies, but another perspective to consider is...
Romans 12:14 KJV
Bless them which persecute you: bless, and curse not.

Curse not the king, no not in thy thought; and curse not the rich in thy bedchamber: for a bird of the air shall carry the voice, and that which hath wings shall tell the matter.
