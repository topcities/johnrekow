A ram's curved horn is hollow and can be made into a loud horn instrument that can be blown like a bugle trumpet.
A ram's curved horn is known as a shophar in the Hebrew language.
It is pronounced, Show-Far.
The sizes and lengths can typically be one to four feet long, or more.
The word, Shophar, is mentioned 72 times in the Old Testament.
In the KJV, it usually gets translated into the word, trumpet.
We see it mentioned many times in Joshua chapter 6,
and in Judges chapter 7.
There were a few times that this word, shophar, was translated into the English word,
horn, and the word, cornet.
In the New Testament, we see 11 occurrences of a Greek word that was translated into
trumpet or trump.
That would be the Greek word,
Sall-pink.
The New Testament book that mentions a Sall-pink, or trump or trumpet, the most often,
would be the book of Revelation, especially in chapter 8.

The TS2009 translation decided to keep the Hebrew word, Shophar, as it is.

Is Hebrews 13:5 trying to quote from Deuteronomy 31:8?
If the Hebrew word that became, fail, could also mean, leave, then maybe, in the Hebrew, it might be a better match than these English translations.
If the book of Hebrews was written in Hebrew, then translated into Greek, then translated into English, maybe it used to match up before all of these translations were made.

Deuteronomy 31:8 -- he will not fail thee
The word, fail -- that Hebrew word right there is
Raw-Faw
and, in the Strong's, and in our other usual Old Testament reference books mentioned on several previous occasions,
looking it up, we see that, Raw-Faw, means,
drop, abandon, forsake, let go or let alone, fail, leave.

So, Hebrews 13:5 could be a valid quote from Deuteronomy 31:8
because the Hebrew word, Raw-Faw, can mean, leave
and the next word, Aw Zav, that became, forsake, that original Hebrew word has a similar kind of meaning...
Aw Zav, means,
to leave, abandon, forsake, neglect,
let loose, set free, let go,
to be forsaken, to be deserted
