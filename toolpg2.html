Troubleshooting a Mac App that Keeps Crashing

The abbreviated steps here are mostly quoted from the following article:

NEWS:  Macworld UK:  Latest Mac Software How To:  10 Ways To Stop Games Crashing On A Mac
http://www.macworld.co.uk/how-to/mac-software/10-reasons-games-crash-on-mac-3600535/

These steps come from the above article, published in March 2015, and are mainly intended for Games that keep crashing,
but these ideas could also be good for troubleshooting any app that might have frequent crashes.

1. Update the app

2. Have Steam Update Automatically

Open Steam for Mac and Control-Click on a game,
choose Properties and click Updates. Set the Automatic Options update to
High Priority - Always auto-update this game ASAP.

3. Check System Requirements Before Troubleshooting

An even better idea is to check this before purchasing.
Compare the app's system requirements with the hardware specifications of your machine.

4. Update the OS X

open App Store and click Updates

5. Adjust app settings

Graphical or other settings can be turned down to enable faster performance,
and higher compatibility with older computers. It may be that the game's (or app's) settings are too high.

Reducing the visual experience may improve the performance and stability.

6. Run as Admin

System Preferences > Users and the Lock icon.
Pick your account and select Allow User to Administer This Computer.
Turning on Admin rights turns off parental controls.

7. Reset your NVRAM

Resetting "non-volatile random-access memory" or NVRAM
On older Mac computers, similar information was stored in Parameter RAM (PRAM).
Resetting NVRAM on Intel-based Macs is done by using the same key combination as resetting PRAM.

Here is how to reset the NVRAM:
Shut down your Mac.
(The Command Key symbol has a shape similar to that of a four-leaf clover.)
Locate the following keys on the keyboard: Command, Option, P, and R.
Turn on your Mac.
Press and hold the Command-Option-P-R keys immediately after you hear the startup sound.
Hold these keys until the computer restarts and you hear the startup sound for a second time.
Release the keys.
After resetting NVRAM, you may need to reconfigure settings for speaker volume, screen resolution, startup disk selection, and time zone information.


8. Update Java

Installing Java on a Mac? Watch out for the now-bundled Ask.com toolbar.
Read and click carefully during the install process to avoid accidental installation of the Ask.com toolbar.


9. Clear cache
found in the Caches folder inside your User/System folder.
Choose Go > Go To Folder and entering ~/Library/Caches.


10. Reinstall the app

The article said, "When deleting the game (or app) we find it's best to use AppZapper, which clears out the main game file and also all of the supporting files. Be careful to check the game's support website for information about deleting and reinstalling the game, and in particular look for any information about transferring any game save data from one installation to the next."

Mac Application Firewall Configuration

Configuring the application firewall in OS X v10.6 and later
Use these steps to enable the application firewall:

Choose System Preferences from the Apple menu.
Click Security or Security & Privacy.
Click the Firewall tab.
Unlock the pane by clicking the lock in the lower-left corner and enter the administrator username and password.
Click "Turn On Firewall" or "Start" to enable the firewall.
Click Advanced to customize the firewall configuration.

Firewall settings do not affect AppleTalk connections.  The firewall may be set to block incoming ICMP "pings" by enabling Stealth Mode in Advanced Settings.  Earlier ipfw technology is still accessible from the command line (in Terminal) and the application firewall does not overrule any rules set using ipfw.  If ipfw blocks an incoming packet, the application firewall does not process it.

Macintosh Troubleshooting for Older Machines

Some of these steps may still work on new Macs.

When troubleshooting an older Mac machine, here are some things to try:

Think of COSP COPR.

Begin by resetting the Power Management Unit (PMU):
Turn off the Macintosh.
While it is off, hold down the following keys for eight seconds:
Command
Option
Shift
Power-Button

A different, longer beep sound may occur.

Next, press the Power-button. As soon as you hear the
Startup chime, hold down the following keys right away, all at the same time, until you hear
the Startup Chime again:
Command
Option
letter P
letter R

This is known as resetting the Parameter RAM (PRAM) chip.

See if your problem has now been solved.


If your computer still does not start up, there is one more thing to try.

Safe Boot
(Newer Macintosh models may not have this feature, or may have steps different than this.)
Turn off the Macintosh.
Wait 10 seconds.
Turn it back on, and hold down the Shift key for a few minutes.
You will need to hold it down for a long time, about two or three minutes, until you see the words, "Safe Boot" on your screen.
You will typically need a password at this point.
See first if it will Login with the password field left empty and blank.


Once your computer has started up,
go to the Applications Folder,
then go into the Utilities Folder,
then double-click on Disk Utility.

Start with the first hard drive listed on the left.

Select the hard drive on the left side.
If the Repair Disk button is not grayed-out, click on it.
Wait for the Repair Disk operation to complete, if you were able to click on it.
Please continue, even if you could not repair the disk.
Next, click on the "Repair Disk Permissions" button.
Once that is completed, go back to the list of hard drives on the left
and select the next hard drive in the list.
Click on the Repair Disk button if it is highlighted, and repeat the above steps
on every hard drive in the list.

Enable the Root User in OS X

The following text comes from the CNET article found at:
http://reviews.cnet.com/8301-13727_7-57617369-263/two-ways-to-enable-the-root-user-in-os-x/?part=rss&subj=news&tag=title


Besides the user account you use to log into the system, OS X contains a number of other accounts, most of which are hidden, but are used for running specific background tasks and system features. One of these accounts is called the "root" account, which is the main overarching system account that has unrestricted access to all system resources. Most of the system's background tasks are run under the root account, as can be seen if you open the Activity Monitor utility and choose "All Processes" from the View menu.
When a program or process is run under the "root" account, it will not be restricted by the permissions settings that prevent a normal user account from accessing potentially critical parts of the system. Where you might normally be met with an access warning, the root account will not and will be given direct and full access to all aspects of the system.
Because of this, the root account can be used to make system-level changes, that would otherwise require you to run special commands in Terminal, or otherwise authenticate when running within your user account. If you are in a situation where you might need to make extensive changes to your system, enabling and logging into the root account might make these changes far easier to do, and you might be specifically instructed to use this account by the troubleshooting resources you are using.
You can use two methods to enable and use this account in OS X:
1. Directory Utility
The tool "Directory Utility" that is relatively hidden in the
Macintosh HD > System > Library > CoreServices folder,
is the default way to enable the root user. Go to this folder and open the program,
then click the lock to authenticate. After you have done this, you can choose
"Enable root user" from the Edit menu.

2. Terminal
The OS X Terminal application is more readily available than
Directory Utility, and can be used to enable the root user by running the following command:

dsenableroot

After you have run either of these two options, you can log out of your account and use "root" as the username of an account to log into. This will then log you in with /private/var as your home folder (instead of a folder within the /Users directory), and you should be able to navigate the entire hard drive and make changes to files and folders without restriction.
Keep in mind that while the root account has this convenience, its unrestricted status makes it a dangerous account to work in, especially since programs and tasks you run might make changes to the system that would otherwise require interaction and authentication. While for the most part simply running in the root account will not be much different, if an application run in this account goes awry, then root access for this program can increase the chance of data corruption and other problems that could affect the system.

FYI:  CD-R and DVD-R Capacity Limits:
The Formatted Capacity of a 703 MB CD Disc Is ACTUALLY 700 MB.
The Formatted Capacity of a single-layer 4.7 GB DVD Disc Is ACTUALLY 4.29 GB.
The Formatted Capacity of a dual-layer 8.5 GB DVD Disc Is ACTUALLY 7.95 GB.

As a Video DVD, the single-layer can hold two hours, and the dual-layer can hold four hours of video.

The hours can be multiplied with a loss of quality.
For example, with extreme compression, some users have said to have placed up to twenty hours of video on a single-layer DVD, with extremely poor picture quality.


Blu-Ray Capacity Limits as of 2008:
A single-layer BD-R disc can hold 25GB.
A Dual-Layer BD-R disc can hold 50GB.
A Quad-Layer BD-R disc can hold 100GB. (availability and compatibility is limited)

A Dual-Layer BD-R disc can fit over 9 hours of high-definition (HD) video on a 50GB disc.
A Dual-Layer BD-R disc can fit over 23 hours of standard-definition (SD) video on a 50GB disc.

As of 2008, the Quad-Layer BD-R disc is very new, and may only be compatible with Quad-Layer players and burners.
Quad-Layer discs may not work in older Blu-Ray players and burners.

In 2009, the fastest burn speed will be moving up to 12X, which can write an entire disc in just 10 minutes.

FYI:  What to do when the Word Spell Checker Stops Working
Before you are finished working on a Word document, type a misspelled word on purpose.  It should underline the misspelled word in red.  If it does not, then there are two steps to make sure the spell checker is on so that there are no other misspelled words in your document.
In Word 2010, go to the File menu, Options, Proofing.  Make sure "Check spelling as you type" is ticked, and at the bottom, make sure "Hide spelling errors in this document" is not ticked. Double check the other settings in this area.  Click OK to save changes.
Next, click the Review tab.  Select all of the text (Ctrl+A).   All of the text in the document is now highlighted.  Click Language and Set Proofing Language.  There is a tick box for "Do not check spelling or grammar."  If this box shows a solid colour, then this means that at least some of your text is being ignored by the spell checker.  Click this box until it shows an empty box that is not ticked.  Click OK.  Since you had selected all of your text, then all of the text will now be checked for correct spelling and grammar.

FYI:  Remove Traces of Inserted USB Devices


Every time you connect a USB device (Flash drive, Thumb drive, USB stick, external hard drive, etc.) to your computer, it creates registry entries and leaves traces behind.
This information can include names, model numbers, and even unique, individual serial numbers.
Over a period of time, you may have connected many different USB or Flash drives to your Windows computer.
Every time you did so, it created additional registry entries, proving that those USB devices were at one time connected to your machine.

You can go into the registry and remove those entries yourself, or you can have an app do it for you.
Be careful with these apps, because, if you remove the wrong items, your machine will forget how to use or communicate with that piece of hardware or device.
Remember, if you remove a USB device entry from the registry, you need to restart your machine straight away.
If you do not restart, your machine will not know how to connect or communicate with the deleted item, and it will not try to do so.
After you restart your machine, it will then try to learn about and connect to the hardware or device entry if it is re-inserted or re-attached again.



Most such traces are usually left behind in the following registry keys:

System\controlset\
CurrentControlSet\Control\DeviceContainers
CurrentControlSet\Control\DeviceClasses
Software\Microsoft\Windows\Shell\MuiCache
All these traces pile up, and even registry cleaners may miss them.

These entries can let others view which USB drives had been connected to your PC. One such tool that lets you do so easily is USBDeview from Nirsoft. It is a small utility that lists all USB devices that are currently in use and those which have been previously used on your computer.
USBDeview is freeware that lets you view all the connected USB devices to your PC.
In addition to this, it also lets you view the history of previously connected USB devices.
