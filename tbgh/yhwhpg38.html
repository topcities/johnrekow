Phil Wickham's Singalong series is a collection of stripped-down live worship albums
recorded in front of large congregations, capturing the sound of communal singing
rather than polished studio production.
Spanning from 2010 to 2019, the albums feature Wickham leading crowds with just guitar and voice,
blending his own well-known songs with beloved Christian worship songs and historic hymns from other artists.
These recordings emphasise participation, with thousands of voices joining in,
creating a raw, immersive worship atmosphere.
Across all four albums, the focus remains on simplicity, authenticity, and shared faith expression through music.

As of March of 2026, there are four of these live albums...

Singalong
Released on 31 August 2010


Singalong 2
Released on 30 October 2012


Singalong 3
Released on 12 May 2015


Singalong 4
Released on 3 May 2019


Here is a brief look at each album...

Singalong
Released on 31 August 2010

Although released in 2010, the original Singalong was actually recorded on 8 August 2008
at Solid Rock Church in Portland, Oregon, in front of around 3,000 attendees.
The album features early fan favourites like "Cannons," "Divine Romance," and "Jesus Lord of Heaven,"
largely drawn from his first two albums.
The sound is intimate and energetic, driven by crowd participation.
This first instalment sets the tone for the entire series: minimal instrumentation, strong congregational singing,
and a focus on worship rather than performance.



Singalong 2
Released on 30 October 2012

Singalong 2 was recorded live at Calvary Chapel Costa Mesa in Santa Ana, California.
Whilst the exact recording date isn't widely documented,
it took place during a single live worship event prior to its October 2012 release.
The album includes songs like "At Your Name (Yahweh, Yahweh)," "Heaven Fall Down," and "The Victory."
Compared to the first album, it expands slightly in scope but keeps the same acoustic, congregational feel.
It also mixes Wickham originals with widely sung worship songs, encouraging audience participation throughout.




Singalong 3
Released on 12 May 2015

Publicly available details about the exact recording date and venue for Singalong 3 are limited,
but it was recorded during a live worship gathering similar in style to the previous albums,
likely in California where Wickham frequently hosts such events.
The album includes popular songs like "This Is Amazing Grace," "Spirit of God,"
and other modern worship anthems.
It continues the established pattern of blending Wickham's own material with broader church worship songs,
emphasising a powerful, unified crowd sound.




Singalong 4
Released on 3 May 2019

Singalong 4 was recorded during a live worship night in Southern California,
though the exact venue and date are not widely specified.
This instalment features well-known songs such as "Living Hope" and "Till I Found You,"
along with appearances from artists like Matt Redman and members of Bethel Music.
The album reflects a fuller, more collaborative worship environment whilst still maintaining the
raw, singalong-driven style of the series.


As of March of 2026, there has been no official announcement of a Singalong 5.
However, Wickham continues to run "Singalong Tour" events (including recent tours in 2023 through 2025),
which follow the same concept, suggesting the format is still active and popular.
Given the popularity of the series, another instalment remains possible, but, as of March of 2026, nothing has been formally confirmed.

The hymn, Spirit of the Living God, Fall Afresh on Me is widely attributed to Daniel Iverson, who wrote both the words and the music in 1926. Unlike many hymns in which the lyrics and melody are created by different individuals, this piece was composed entirely by Iverson himself. The musical tune is often identified in hymnals as "Iverson."

Daniel Iverson was born in 1890 in Brunswick, Georgia, United States, and later became an ordained Presbyterian minister in 1914. His education included studies at the University of Georgia, Moody Bible Institute in Chicago, Columbia Theological Seminary, and the University of South Carolina. Throughout his career he served Presbyterian congregations in several southern states, including Georgia, North Carolina, and South Carolina. In 1927 he founded Shenandoah Presbyterian Church in Miami, Florida, where he served as pastor for approximately twenty-four years until 1951. Iverson continued preaching after retirement and died in Asheville, North Carolina in 1977 at the age of eighty-six. Although he wrote other religious songs and materials, the hymn Spirit of the Living God, Fall Afresh on Me became his most widely known work.

The hymn itself dates to February 1926, meaning that it is about one century old as of March of 2026. It emerged during a period when short gospel choruses were commonly written for revival meetings and evangelistic gatherings. Its brevity and prayer-like language contributed to its enduring popularity in churches across many denominations.

The hymn has generally been known by its opening line, "Spirit of the Living God, Fall Afresh on Me." As with many devotional choruses of the early twentieth century, the first line effectively served as the title. Later hymnals continued to use that opening phrase as the identifying title of the song.

After its composition in 1926, the hymn circulated informally for a few years. It was later published in the hymnal Revival Songs in 1929, compiled by the gospel music publisher Robert H. Coleman. Early printings occasionally appeared without Iverson's name, which briefly obscured authorship, but subsequent hymnals restored proper credit to him as both lyricist and composer.

The circumstances surrounding the hymn's first performance are closely tied to a revival meeting in Orlando, Florida. During a campaign led by evangelist George T. Stephans and his team, Iverson attended a service and heard a sermon focused on the work of the Holy Spirit. The message made a profound impression. Later that day, Iverson sat at a piano and wrote the brief hymn as a personal prayer. The pianist associated with the evangelistic team quickly copied the melody and words onto manuscript paper, and the song leader introduced it to the congregation that very evening. As a result, the first public performance of the hymn occurred on the same day it was written.

The hymn's creation was therefore directly connected to a particular spiritual experience during that revival gathering. Rather than being written as part of a planned musical project, it arose spontaneously in response to the sermon's theme about spiritual renewal and the activity of the Holy Spirit in believers' lives. The simplicity of the lyrics reflects this spontaneous origin, giving the text the character of a brief prayer set to music.

Biblical language strongly influenced the wording of the hymn. The phrase "Spirit of the living God" closely echoes Second Epistle to the Corinthians 3:3, which refers to believers as letters written not with ink but with the Spirit of the living God. That scriptural wording helped shape the central line repeated throughout the hymn.

Additional biblical passages are often associated with the hymn's themes. The idea of the Spirit "falling" recalls passages in Acts of the Apostles, such as Acts 10:44, where the Holy Spirit falls upon listeners to the gospel. The imagery of being moulded or shaped reflects the biblical metaphor of the potter and the clay, found in texts such as Book of Jeremiah 18:1-6 and echoed in the apostle Paul's writings. The hymn's focus on transformation through the Spirit also resonates with themes expressed in Epistle to the Romans and other New Testament passages.

The earliest known form of the hymn contained only one short stanza. In that original version, the lyrics read:
"Spirit of the living God, fall fresh on me;
Spirit of the living God, fall fresh on me;
Break me, melt me, mould me, fill me;
Spirit of the living God, fall fresh on me."

Later hymnals commonly altered the wording "fall fresh on me" to "fall afresh on me," which eventually became the standard wording.

A second stanza sometimes sung today was not written by Iverson. That additional verse was composed in 1980 by Michael Baughen, an Anglican bishop and hymn writer. Baughen's text expands the prayer into a corporate appeal for unity, love, humility, and service amongst believers. His verse was first published together with Iverson's original chorus in hymnals during the early 1980s.

Over time the hymn gained widespread recognition because of its unusual simplicity. The melody is very short and easy to learn, and the text contains only a few lines repeated as a prayer. These characteristics allowed congregations to memorise it quickly and use it during moments of reflection or response in worship services. The song eventually appeared in more than a hundred hymnals and became a staple in many Christian traditions.

In summary, Spirit of the Living God, Fall Afresh on Me is a short devotional hymn written in 1926 by Presbyterian minister Daniel Iverson after he attended a revival meeting in Orlando, Florida. Both the words and music were composed by Iverson and introduced to a congregation on the very day they were written. The hymn later appeared in the 1929 hymnal Revival Songs and spread widely through churches because of its simple melody and heartfelt prayer for spiritual renewal. A second verse written decades later by Michael Baughen expanded the song's message of unity and love, but Iverson's original stanza remains the core of the century-old hymn that continues to be sung in worship today.

The widely sung gospel chorus known as, "I've Got a River of Life," sometimes titled "There's a River of Life" or simply "River of Life," has long been attributed to a songwriter identified as L. Casebolt, often assumed to be Louis Casebolt. Hymnals, recordings, and lyric collections frequently credit the song to this name, though usually only as an initial and surname. Many hymn databases list the composition as "Words and Music by L. Casebolt," suggesting that the same individual may have been responsible for both the lyrics and melody of the original chorus. Despite the song's enduring popularity in churches, relatively little verified biographical information about Casebolt has been preserved.

Evidence from several hymn collections and music publications suggests that the song eventually involved more than one contributor. Some recordings and songbooks credit both L. Casebolt and Betty Carr Pulkingham. Research into early performances and printed hymn collections indicates that the opening verse and basic refrain may have originated with Casebolt, whilst additional verses appearing in later versions were composed by Pulkingham. As the song circulated more widely in worship settings, later publications began crediting both individuals together, reflecting the expanded lyrical form that became common in hymnbooks and recordings.

Available copyright listings and hymnbook references place the origin of the song in the early 1970s, during a period of rapid growth in contemporary praise music within charismatic Christian communities. Some copyright records mention dates such as 1971 and 1975, associated with worship music publishers connected to the emerging praise-chorus movement. The song also appeared in the influential Scripture in Song hymnbook series during the 1970s, a publication that played a major role in spreading new worship songs internationally amongst evangelical and charismatic congregations.

The identity of Louis Casebolt remains somewhat mysterious. Although the name appears regularly in song credits, reliable biographical information about the individual has proven difficult to locate. Many hymn lyric websites and song archives acknowledge that the composer is credited as "L. Casebolt", but state that little documented information about the person has survived. It has often been suggested that Casebolt may have been a preacher or pastor active during the mid-twentieth century, though this claim is not confirmed by widely accessible historical records.

What can be said with some confidence is that Casebolt appears to have been active in circles connected with the charismatic worship movement of the late twentieth century. His name appears in the credits of the "River of Life" chorus and occasionally in praise music collections from that era, but little else is documented about his life, ministry, or musical career. There are currently no well-verified sources that establish a birth date, death date, place of birth, or confirmed pastoral position associated with the songwriter.

In contrast, the collaborator sometimes credited alongside Casebolt, Betty Carr Pulkingham, is a much better documented figure in the history of contemporary Christian worship. Pulkingham was a worship leader and songwriter associated with Church of the Redeemer in Houston, Texas, a congregation known for its role in the charismatic renewal movement of the 1960s and 1970s. Through her involvement in worship leadership and the publication of praise song collections, she contributed to the spread of many simple congregational choruses that became staples of charismatic worship gatherings.

The memorable line "Spring up, O well" found in the song's refrain draws inspiration from John 4:14, where Jesus speaks of living water becoming a well that springs up to eternal life. Over time, the refrain developed several variations as the song was taught informally in church services, youth gatherings, and Christian camps. Some versions use the phrase "Spring up, O well within my soul," whilst others substitute "within my spirit." In certain children's adaptations, playful phrases such as "gush, gush" or "splish, splash" were added as rhythmic actions accompanying hand motions.

Documented appearances of the song in printed form can be traced to the 1970s, particularly within the Scripture in Song series of worship songbooks. These publications helped introduce the chorus to congregations across North America, the United Kingdom, Australia, and other regions. The books became a key vehicle for distributing new praise music during the charismatic movement, allowing songs like "River of Life" to spread far beyond their original local settings.

Early recordings also helped popularise the song. One cited example is the 1977 "Songs of Praise - Album 3" recording, which credited both L. Casebolt and Betty Pulkingham. Another recording, "Rejoice with the Fisherfolk" (1978), attributed the first verse to Casebolt and additional verses to Pulkingham. These recordings demonstrate that the song had already developed multiple textual layers by the late 1970s.

The existence of several lyric variations can largely be explained by the way the song circulated. Praise choruses of the early charismatic movement were frequently transmitted through live worship gatherings rather than through formal sheet-music publication. As a result, songs were often adapted, shortened, expanded, or modified as they spread from congregation to congregation. Children's ministry leaders and camp counsellors sometimes added sound effects and motions, creating the well-known playful versions that include phrases like "gush, gush" or "splish splash."

A number of factors help explain why the original composer remains so obscure despite the song's popularity. Many early praise choruses emerged from grassroots church movements rather than professional music publishing systems. In addition, early songbooks sometimes listed writers only by initials, and documentation from the early charismatic era was not always carefully preserved. If Casebolt composed only a small number of songs, it would further reduce the likelihood that detailed biographical records would have been widely archived.

Taken together, the available evidence suggests that "River of Life" likely originated around 1970 or 1971, with L. Casebolt responsible for the original chorus and possibly the melody. Betty Carr Pulkingham appears to have contributed additional verses and helped spread the song through the charismatic worship movement of the 1970s. Over time, the chorus evolved into multiple lyrical forms, including expanded hymn versions and children's action adaptations. Despite the song's widespread use in churches and camps, the life and background of Louis Casebolt remain largely undocumented, leaving the composer of one of the era's most recognisable praise choruses an intriguing historical mystery.

From what was found on Wikipedia, the free encyclopaedia,
back in March of 2026, "I Surrender All" is a Christian hymn,
with words written by American art teacher and musician Judson W. Van DeVenter (1855 - 1939),
who subsequently became a music minister and evangelist.
It was put to music by Winfield S. Weeden (1847 - 1908), and published in 1896.

"I Surrender All" was put to music by Weeden,
and first published in 1896 in a songbook, titled, Gospel Songs of Grace and Glory,
a collection of old and new hymns by various hymnists,
compiled by Weeden, Van DeVenter, and Leonard Weaver, and published by Sebring Publishing Co.

In the late 1930s, when popular international evangelist Billy Graham
was a student at Florida Bible Institute,
he studied and fellowshipped with Judson Van DeVenter,
and later stated that Van DeVenter greatly influenced his early preaching.
Graham subsequently popularised "I Surrender All"
in his massive crusades and revivals, beginning in the late 1940s.

The song, I Surrender All, was recorded by many artists, including...
Mahalia Jackson (but not released until 2005)
Little Richard (1979)
Deniece Williams (1986)
Glen Campbell (1989)
Cece Winans (1995)
Carman (1997)
Amy Grant (2005)
Michael W. Smith (2008)
Newsboys (2014)
Carrie Underwood (2021)
