Did Goliath have any brothers? Yes, he did. Goliath and his brothers can be found here:

2 Samuel 21:18-22 (NIV):
"In the course of time, there was a war with the Philistines at Gob. At that time, Sibbekai the Hushathite killed Saph, one of the descendants of the giants. "In the course of the war against the Philistines, Elhanan son of Jair killed Lahmi the brother of Goliath, who was from Gath. The shaft of his spear was like a weaver's rod. Commentary:
These passages indicate that Goliath had brothers who were also giants, specifically mentioning that Lahmi was a brother of Goliath. The term "descendants of Rapha" refers to the giants, which implies that Goliath and his brothers were part of this lineage.


The identification of the individuals mentioned in the passages as brothers of Goliath is based on the context and wording used in the biblical texts. Here's how the connections are made:

Contextual Clues:
In both 2 Samuel 21:18-22 and 1 Chronicles 20:4-8, the narrative discusses battles involving the Philistines and mentions Goliath specifically. The passages refer to other giants who are described as being from Gath, the same city as Goliath, and they are noted as descendants of Rapha, which is a term often associated with giants.

Direct Mention:
In 1 Chronicles 20:4, it explicitly states that Elhanan killed "Lahmi the brother of Goliath." This direct mention establishes a familial relationship.

Similar Descriptions:
The other giants mentioned in these passages are described in ways that align with the characteristics of Goliath, such as their size and lineage. This suggests they are part of the same family or group of giants.

Repetition of Themes:
The mention of multiple giants in the context of battles against Israel, along with the specific reference to Goliath's brother, reinforces the idea that these individuals are related.

Whilst the texts do not provide a detailed family tree, the combination of these contextual clues and direct references leads to the conclusion that the giants mentioned in these passages are indeed brothers or close relatives of Goliath.

There was a 1967 song that became very popular in 1969.
It was called, "Oh Happy Day."
The original version was first released in 1755,
coming from clergyman Philip Doddridge. That 1755 hymn was then put to music
that was originally released in 1704 by Johann Anastasius Freylinghausen (1670 - 1739).

The melody was then modified in 1854 by Edward Rimbault,
and his version had the title, O Happy Day, That Fixed My Choice.


The exact year Philip Doddridge wrote the hymn "Oh, Happy Day"
is not definitively documented.
However, it is believed that he composed the text
in the early 18th century, likely around the 1730s.


The hymn "Oh, Happy Day" is often attributed to Philip Doddridge,
but it was actually published posthumously.
Doddridge wrote the text of the hymn,
but it was not released until after his death in 1751.
The hymn was included in a collection of his works that was published in 1755.

So, whilst Doddridge did not release the hymn himself,
his work was compiled and published by others after his passing.


To summarise this again,
The original 1755 lyrics were published four years after the death of English Nonconformist, Congregationalist, minister, educator, and hymnwriter, Philip Doddridge (1702 - 1751).
It may have been written sometime during the 1730s, but the exact year is unknown.
The added 1854 lyrics were by British organist, musicologist, book collector and author Edward Rimbault (1816 - 1876),
and the popular 1967 arrangement and lyrics were created by American gospel musician, pianist, vocalist, choir master, composer, and arranger Edwin Hawkins (1943 - 2018).


Edwin Hawkins was one of the originators of the urban contemporary gospel sound.
As the leader of the Edwin Hawkins Singers, he was probably best known for his 1967 arrangement of "Oh Happy Day."

The ouroboros, symbolised by a serpent or dragon eating its own tail, indeed has significant associations with time, often representing cyclicality and eternity.

In philosophical contexts, particularly within ancient Egyptian and Greco-Roman traditions, the ouroboros illustrates the idea of eternal return. This notion implies that history and existence repeat themselves infinitely, highlighting an unending cycle of time.

In alchemy, the ouroboros symbolises the cyclical process of transformation. It reflects the idea that spiritual and physical processes evolve through cycles, emphasising a journey through different states over time.

Eastern philosophies, such as those in Hinduism and Buddhism, often view time in a non-linear fashion, akin to the ouroboros. The symbolism illustrates the interconnection of past, present, and future.

The ouroboros serves as a powerful symbol of time, reflecting concepts of cycles, eternity, and transformation. Its representation invites contemplation on the nature of existence and the passage of time.

Phil Wickham decided to take that song, and keep the main chorus close to its original version, but create brand new lyrics, and give it a new, fresh sound.

On 11 April 2025, Phil Wickham released his updated version of the song, titled "What an Awesome God".
It was later released onto his tenth studio album, Song of the Saints, on 12 September 2025.
The song became his first Billboard Hot 100 entry and topped Billboard's Christian Airplay and AC Airplay charts.


What was it that made Phil Wickham want to make a new version of the song with new lyrics?

Logan Sekulow is the co-editor of CCM Magazine, along with also being a television host and producer, filmmaker, and long-time voice in faith-based media.

https://www.ccmmagazine.com/news/why-phil-wickham-reimagined-awesome-god-exclusive-interview/

Why Phil Wickham Reimagined 'Awesome God' -- Exclusive Interview

Here are a few selected portions being quoted from that article.

Logan mentions, "At first glance you may hear a glossy remake with brand new verses and a modernised version of the historic chorus. After talking to Phil about it though, the motivation was clear. What brought him to Awesome God was reverence, memories, and a surprising kind of joy."

Phil Wickham explains it like this:
Phil Wickham says, "I was in Nashville in my buddy's studio, Jonathan Smith...it was 11, 11:30 at night, and me and a couple guys in the studio were just talking about songs that we loved as kids."

"So we thought, well, man, what about Awesome God? It'd be great to throw in at the end of a song. And then we started joking around about, like, what if we wrote new verses because we never could sing the verses in the first place?"

"Someone turned the computer back on, played a beat, I sang a melody, and then 30 minutes later had the song written for What an Awesome God. We just had a fun time, turned it off at 1 a.m., went to bed, woke up the next morning, listened to it, and we all kind of looked at each other thinking like, this kind of hits. Like, this is kind of awesome."

"I just put it on social media to get a gauge because I don't want to ruin this," Phil said. "I don't want to create a bad situation here, like ruining a song that people love."

The reaction was instant. "I put it up on social media, and by far, of anything I've ever posted, it got like crazy feedback of people just saying, 'We're so excited. Bring it back. This is something we want to sing again.' And so I just let the audience decide."

"...I like the old verses too. I'm definitely not trying to replace anything, but more honour a song and bring a chorus into it. It's been so life-giving to bring these choruses into nights again and just remind myself of the first time I met God through these songs."
