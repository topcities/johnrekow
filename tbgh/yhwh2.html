KJV: Write the things which thou hast seen, and the things which are, and the things which shall be hereafter;

NLT: "Write down what you have seen--both the things that are now happening and the things that will happen.


Note the sequence in how the information is being presented, in how the verse is worded. It is strictly in chronological order, presented and documented in the precise sequence as it shall happen in time.

1. thou hast seen -- the past events, The things which you have seen -- Chapter 1 -- The Lord's Person -- The revelation of the Resurrected Jesus

2. The things which are -- the present and current events -- Chapters 2-3 – The Lord's People - Church history

3. which shall be hereafter -- the events that are going to happen in the future -- The things which will take place after this – Chapters 4-22 -- The Lord's Program -- What's coming up, the rapture, tribulation and the forever-after happy ending.

The author was being clearly commanded that the information being presented to him is to be kept strictly in the same order in which it is presented so that it maintains the exact order in which the events shall happen in time, from the past, into the present, and then into the future.

Ch. 1 -- What John had seen, the vision of Jesus.

Ch. 2 & 3 -- The things which are, the letters to the churches, represented as the seven candlesticks, or seven lampstands.

Ch. 4 & 5 -- The Rapture -- We go to Heaven -- the lamps of fire, or, the seven lampstands are now in Heaven, now representing the Seven Sprits of God.

Ch. 6 through 20 -- God's wrath is activated and poured out upon a Christ-rejecting, sinful world

Ch. 20 and 21 -- and they all lived happily forever after

I would like to point out that, they did have many more topics in addition to the ones that we looked at here.

I say this because, I was disappointed a few times by some of their summary statements. I would argue that a few of their summaries did not accurately communicate the original Bible verse that they were supposed to be referring to.

That's why it's always better to read the verses, chapters and stories directly out of the Bible, instead of trying to condense and summarise it down.

Commentary:
I have a thing called, the 10-minute rule. If a pastor talks more than 10 minutes without actually quoting or reading a verse directly out of Bible, watch out, as he may start to go off track and lose his accuracy and precision, and as a result, he may start to say things that may not be in alignment with what the Bible teaches.

Is this a Bible study, or is this a survey of contemporary literature, quoting all sorts of famous authors and people, but not actually reading quotes directly out of the Bible?
